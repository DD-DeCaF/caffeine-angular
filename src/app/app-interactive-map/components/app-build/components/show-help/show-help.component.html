Modeling methods

Constraint-based modeling

The following simulation methods predict metabolic fluxes that optimize certain biological objectives, for example, growth yield. They operate under the assumption that the organism’s metabolism is in a dynamic equilibrium (pseudo steady-state): internal metabolite concentrations are constant and production and consumption fluxes perfectly balance each other.
This is a reasonable assumption when the environment is constant (e.g. in a chemostat) or there is nutrient excess (e.g. a microbial culture growing in exponential phase).

Physiological data (uptake and secretion rates, yields, growth rates, C13 fluxes etc.) can all be readily integrated in the form of reaction rate constraints. In the future, the integration of additional types of omics data will be added.

You can find a comprehensive overview of constraint-based modeling and its applications here.

Flux Balance Analysis (FBA)

Flux balance analysis optimizes a biological objective to predict a distribution of metabolic fluxes. The default objective is the maximization of growth rate.

Caveats:

  • In general, flux distributions predicted through FBA are not unique. Uncertainties in the predicted fluxes can be assessed through Flux Variability Analysis (see also below).
  • Biomass composition is assumed to be constant and growth-rate independent.
  • The objective is optimization of growth yield and not growth rate.

Parsimonious FBA (recommended)

In addition to maximizing growth yield (see FVA below), Parsimonious Flux Balance Analysis (pFBA) will simultaneously minimize the sum of all fluxes which is a proxy for minimizing enzyme expression.

Caveats:

  • Flux minimization is only a proxy for the minimization of protein expression cost. For more accurate modeling approaches to proteome allocation, please refer to the GECKO method (already supported for S. cerevisae) and ME models (which will be made available soon).

Flux Variability Analysis (FVA)

In general, constraint-based modeling solutions will have many alternative optima. Flux Variability Analysis (FVA) allows the estimation of the uncertainty in flux predictions computed by FBA. Uncertain reaction rates are highlighted using transparency.

Parsimonious FVA

Similar to FVA, parsimonious FVA will estimate the uncertainty of flux predictions computed by pFBA.
